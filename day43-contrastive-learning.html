DeepLearningFromZero · Day 43

🧲 Contrastive Learning — every day 43 taught a net to classify by showing it labels. But labels are expensive, and the internet is a bottomless well of unlabelled images. Contrastive learning (the heart of SimCLR) learns a useful representation with no labels at all, from one deceptively simple idea: take an image, make two random augmentations of it (crop, flip, colour-jitter) — these two views are a positive pair that must mean the same thing — and treat every other image in the batch as a negative. Train an encoder so the two views of one item land close together in embedding space while the negatives are pushed apart. The loss that does this is InfoNCE (a.k.a. NT-Xent): for an anchor, form the cosine similarity to every other embedding, divide by a temperature τ, and take a softmax — then maximise the weight on the true positive: L = −log( esim(z,z⁺)/τ / Σk esim(z,zk)/τ ). It is literally a classification loss where the “class” is which sample is my positive. The magic: because augmentations of a cat still look catlike, the encoder is forced to keep whatever is invariant to the augmentation — the content — so genuinely similar items cluster together even though it never saw a single label. Drop the negatives and the whole thing collapses to a single point; the temperature τ is the knob that decides how hard the negatives are fought.

🔨 Build it yourself

    
SimCLR is remarkably small: an augmentation that turns one image into two views, an encoder + projection head that maps a view to a normalised embedding, and the NT-Xent / InfoNCE loss that pulls the two views of an item together while pushing every other view away. We assemble it one line at a time — the augmentation, the encoder, L2-normalisation, the similarity matrix, the temperature-scaled cross-entropy, a full training step, and finally how you actually use the frozen encoder afterward.

    
    
      
  1. 
        
    1
    Augment — one image becomes two views (the positive pair)
    
        
    The only “supervision” is a strong stochastic augmentation. Apply it twice to the same image to get two views that we define as a positive pair — they should mean the same thing. SimCLR found the mix matters enormously: random resized crop + colour jitter + grayscale + blur. Without colour jitter the net cheats by matching colour histograms instead of content.
    
        
    import torch, torch.nn as nn, torch.nn.functional as F
from torchvision import transforms as T

aug = T.Compose([
    T.RandomResizedCrop(96, scale=(0.2, 1.0)),
    T.RandomHorizontalFlip(),
    T.RandomApply([T.ColorJitter(0.4,0.4,0.4,0.1)], p=0.8),
    T.RandomGrayscale(p=0.2),
    T.GaussianBlur(9),
    T.ToTensor(),
])
def two_views(img):
    return aug(img), aug(img)     # v1, v2 : the positive pair

    Encoder + projection head
    
        
    A backbone f (e.g. a ResNet) maps a view to a feature h; a small MLP projection head g maps h to the space where the loss lives. Crucially, the contrastive loss is applied on g(h), but the representation you keep for downstream tasks is h — the head is thrown away. This detail measurably improves the learned features.
    
        
    class SimCLR(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, backbone, feat_dim, proj_dim=128):
        super().__init__()
        self.f = backbone                       # e.g. ResNet, no classifier
        self.g = nn.Sequential(                 # projection head (MLP)
            nn.Linear(feat_dim, feat_dim), nn.ReLU(),
            nn.Linear(feat_dim, proj_dim))
    def forward(self, x):
        h = self.f(x)                           # representation (KEEP this)
        z = self.g(h)                           # projection (loss lives here)
        return h, z

    L2-normalise — work on the unit hypersphere
    
        
    Normalise every projection to unit length. Now the dot product is the cosine similarity, in [-1, 1], so only the direction of an embedding matters, not its magnitude. This is why our demo can draw the embeddings on a circle: after normalisation that is exactly where they live.
    
        
    z = F.normalize(z, dim=1)          # ||z|| = 1  ->  z·z' = cos angle
# similarity of two embeddings is now just their dot product

    The similarity matrix over the batch
    
        
    Stack both views of all N images into 2N embeddings and compute all pairwise cosine similarities in one matmul. For row i, its positive is its sibling view; the other 2N−2 rows are negatives. Mask the diagonal so an embedding is never its own negative.
    
        
    z = torch.cat([z1, z2], dim=0)          # [2N, d]  (z1,z2 each [N,d])
sim = z @ z.t()                          # [2N, 2N] cosine similarities
mask = torch.eye(2*N, dtype=torch.bool, device=z.device)
sim.masked_fill_(mask, float('-inf'))    # drop self-similarity
# positive of row i:  (i+N) % 2N   -> the other view of the same image

  5. 
        
    5
    InfoNCE / NT-Xent — a softmax that picks the positive
    
        
    Divide the similarities by the temperature τ and treat each row as classification logits whose correct “class” is the index of its positive. Cross-entropy then maximises the softmax weight on the true positive and, by normalisation, suppresses the negatives. That is the whole loss: L = −log( es⁺/τ / Σk esk/τ ).
    
        
    def info_nce(sim, N, tau=0.5):
    logits = sim / tau                      # temperature-scaled
    targets = torch.arange(2*N, device=sim.device)
    targets = (targets + N) % (2*N)         # index of each row's positive
    return F.cross_entropy(logits, targets) # softmax + pick the positive

  6. 
        
    6
    Temperature τ — and why you need negatives
    
        
    A small τ sharpens the softmax so the nearest (hardest) negative dominates the gradient — strong, sharp separation, but sensitive and prone to breaking apart semantically-close samples. A large τ softens it, spreading the penalty gently. And the negatives are not optional: with positives alone the trivial optimum is to map every input to the same vector — collapse. The denominator’s negatives are the “uniformity” force that keeps the sphere populated.
    
        
    # tau: SimCLR default ~0.1-0.5.  smaller = harder contrast.
# NO negatives  ->  L = -sim(z1, z2)/tau   (alignment only)
#   optimum is f(x) = const for all x  ->  representations COLLAPSE.
# negatives in the denominator add repulsion (uniformity) -> no collapse.

    One training step
    
        
    Put it together: two views → encode both → normalise → similarity matrix → InfoNCE → backward. Big batches help — more negatives per step is a stronger signal — which is why SimCLR is hungry for batch size (later methods like MoCo use a memory queue to fake a large negative set on a small batch).
    
        
    def train_step(x1, x2, model, opt, tau=0.5):
    _, z1 = model(x1); _, z2 = model(x2)     # two views through the net
    z1, z2 = F.normalize(z1,1), F.normalize(z2,1)
    z = torch.cat([z1, z2], 0)
    sim = z @ z.t()
    sim.masked_fill_(torch.eye(len(z),dtype=bool,device=z.device), -9e15)
    loss = info_nce(sim, x1.size(0), tau)
    opt.zero_grad(); loss.backward(); opt.step()
    return loss.item()

    Use it — the linear probe, and the family it spawned
    
        
    After pretraining, freeze the encoder and train a single linear layer on top with the few labels you do have — the linear probe. SimCLR’s frozen features rival fully-supervised training. The idea generalises: MoCo (momentum encoder + queue), BYOL/SimSiam (no negatives, avoid collapse with a predictor + stop-gradient), and CLIP (the positive pair is an image and its caption — InfoNCE across modalities, the backbone of modern multimodal models).
    
        
    backbone.eval()                              # freeze the pretrained encoder
for p in backbone.parameters(): p.requires_grad = False
clf = nn.Linear(feat_dim, num_classes)       # a single layer, few labels
# train only clf on (h = backbone(x), label)  -> the "linear probe"

# lineage:  SimCLR -> MoCo (queue) -> BYOL/SimSiam (no negatives)
#           -> CLIP (image<->text InfoNCE)  the multimodal workhorse

🎉 Day 43 of DeepLearningFromZero done.
Contrastive learning turns unlabelled data into a training signal by inventing its own labels: two augmentations of one item are a positive pair that must agree, and every other item in the batch is a negative to push away. The InfoNCE / NT-Xent loss −log( esim/τ / Σ esim/τ ) is just a temperature-scaled softmax that asks “which of these is my positive?” — pulling positives together (alignment) while the negatives in the denominator keep the space spread out (uniformity). Because augmentations preserve content but not nuisance detail, the encoder is forced to keep meaning, so semantically similar items cluster with no labels ever seen — exactly what you watched happen on the circle. Remove the negatives and it collapses; τ tunes how hard the contrast is fought. 👉 Tomorrow, Day 44: CLIP — the same InfoNCE loss, but the positive pair is an image and its caption, learning a shared image–text space you can search and prompt in words.
